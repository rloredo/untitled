A Basque Conundrum

I

— It's always raining nowadays. It hurts, he said.

— I really don't care, she replied.

— You never go out, anyway.

— What for? I'm better here, I don't have anything to do outside. And you bring me all I need. You're so sweet.

— It will make you feel better, maybe you will cheer up a little.

— You just said that it's always raining, so why bother?

She lit another cigarette while he was leaving the apartment. The ashtray was almost full. From her tiny window, she could see, between the newer buildings, a little bit of the sea. She couldn't hear the waves, but she imagined the sound that they make when breaking into the shore.

— You don't want to go out either, do you Iker?

Her cat meowed asking for food.

— Yeah, you want to stay with me, she said, while she put some food on the bowl and the cat purred.

She grabbed a poetry book so worn it was falling appart. The covers were in really bad shape and the pages were full of folds and yellow stains of tobacco. She read out loud one more time the beginning of one of her favorite poems:

Soñutzik ontzi-puntan, sakoneko lemazain,
itxaso-barne nua zidor ezezaunen gain.
Ondartzan oro laga! —arriskubak darraigu—,
Lur-barri billatzale ametsa lagun dogu,
Liorreko lokatzok geure malkoz egiñak.

She fell asleep. The cigarette between her fingers burnt slowly and turned off when it reached her skin. It burned her a little, but that didn't wake her up.

II

A loud knocking woke her up. She was upset, nervous. She kept the chain lock on and opened the door just enough to look out. There was a police officer outside.

— Is the radio too loud? I'm sorry.

— No, I don't mean to bother you miss, it is not the music. Can I ask you some questions? There has been a murder.

She opened the door. A strong smell hit the officer's nose: stale tobacco smoke and cat food layered over the dry, papery scent of old books. He squinted.

— Sorry for the mess, she said. I wasn't expecting visits.

— Have you seen something suspicious? The officer asked.

— No sorry, I don't go out much. I've been tired lately.

The officer took a quick look around the room. A full ashtray, a green book, and a few pills were all that he saw. She didn't ask about the murder but she saw that the policeman was looking at her pills.

— They help me with my sleeping. I have terrible nightmares if I don't take them.

He looked at her with pity. She was very pale and skinny. Her hair didn't look so bad, she was blond and had it tied back with a blue band.

— So, you haven't heard anything in these last hours, right?

— No, sorry, I'm not of much help, I'm afraid.

— Don't worry miss, thank you for your time. If you remember anything just call us.

The officer left and she stared at him while he walked down the corridor.

— Excuse me, officer! She shouted before he went down the stairs.

She hesitated. The question slipped out before she could stop it.

— Who died?

— A man, he said, while he was checking his notebook. Argider, do you know him?

— No sorry, I don't. She replied in a slightly broken voice.

She was lying. He was her only friend.

She went back in and collapsed on the couch. The officer's footsteps faded down the corridor until there was only the building's hush. She cried. It was the first time in a long time. Her cat jumped on her lap to comfort her. She hugged him and fell asleep again. Outside, the rain was tapping at the window.

III

— Here you are Iker, you are really going to enjoy this.

She lit the last cigarette and looked through the window. It was late. She could see the glints from the lighthouse of Igueldo. She stared at them and finished the cigarette. She tried to remain calm but she started to panic. She opened her wardrobe and looked through all her clothes, searching for her raincoat.

She left the apartment and knocked Argider's door. No one answered. She realized that she didn't know a lot about his personal life. Did he have children? Grandchildren? A wife? Was he alone too? No one should be alone in their old age, she thought.

She knew was that he was a sailor and that he was now too old to captain a ship. The arthrosis on his hands didn't let him, but that didn't keep him from smoking his pipe. In the good days, during the summer, he smoked with her while she read to him her favorite poems. He just looked out the window, to the tiny portion of the sea that was visible.

— Do you know that we have very big waves here in Donostia? He told her. Only good sailors can deal with big waves. You have to be brave but a little bit frightened. Is fear what gets you back to the shore. Pure bravery will only get you killed.

— Or disappointed, she said.

— Or disappointed, he repeated agreeing.

She had never been on a ship, although she had lived all her life by the sea.

— How is it? she asked him.

— Well, it is not different than walking when drunk. The floor moves a little, and sometimes you feel like you want to puke.

She laughed. And it was beautiful.

— Oh, come on! be serious. I've never been on a ship, and I've lived my whole life by the sea. Isn't that sad?

— It is. It is very sad. So why don't you just go out, board one, and sail a little?

— Oh, I don't like going out. You know that. And I don't think that I would find something more beautiful than the things that I have in my books.

— You're right on that, but at least they would be real.

Her smile disappeared. Her eyes turned melancholic. Argider regretted his last words. He was trying to think about something that would make her smile again, but he couldn't.

— You know, Argider, I really like you. You're my best friend. Can you tell me a story about one of your trips?

IV

So, when I was younger I worked as a fisherman, you know. Our ship was orange and smelled really bad, but it was my job and I really liked it. One day, we were fishing a few miles from the coast, not very far, because back then it was easier: the fish were everywhere and all we had to do was throw the net and wait for the fish to get tangled up.

The sun was going down and we were taking out the nets, all of them were filled with fish, but one had a chest. A small one, you know, like the one in pirate movies, but smaller. It was covered in seaweed and it was in really bad shape. So we grabbed it and we opened it. Inside there weren't any treasures, only some wet papers and an old bottle of wine.

We kept taking out the rest of the nets, full of fish. It was a good day. When we were coming back to the port, after the exhausting night, I opened the bottle of wine, just out of curiosity. It didn't have any label, and it smelled awful—well, for wine, not for a salad dressing in a fancy restaurant. I was about to throw it all into the sea but the captain shouted:

Man overboard!

That's what you shout when you see a man in the water, even if it's not from your ship. So I dropped the bottle and ran to grab the lifebuoy—the circular orange thing that looks like a donut, you know—and I threw it to the poor guy floating there in the middle of nothing. We took him aboard. He could barely breathe.

We made a radio call to the coast guard to ask if there had been a shipwreck and to let them know that we'd found one man drifting away, but nothing else. We couldn't see any lights. They told us that they hadn't received any mayday calls, the ones you make when your life is at risk, you know, and that they were as surprised as we were.

So this castaway fell asleep and we kept him warm until we got to the port. Once we were anchored, we woke him up and asked how we could help, if any member of his crew was still out there. He didn't say a word. We gave him a coffee and let him rest while we unloaded all the fish.

Then I stumbled upon the wine bottle that was lying on the deck. It was almost empty, and something was shining inside. I tipped the rest onto my hand. Two diamonds fell from the bottle. Two big diamonds.

V

— And what happened to the guy, the one you found? she asked.

— He disappeared while we were working in the docks.

— Really? Just like that? I think that you're being lazy and that you don't want to finish your story, Argider.

— No, he just disappeared in the middle of the night, I swear. He left half of the coffee and took the small chest. But nothing worthy was there, just the wet papers.

— Oh, how mysterious! And the diamonds? Do you still have them? Or did you sell them?

— I still have them, do you want to see them?

— I'd love it!

She smiled and Argider felt well again.

— I'll be right back!

He went down to look for the diamonds. She just stayed there looking out the window. She imagined herself outside, in a ship. She felt a shudder. And sat down before Argider came back.

— Look, they are really big.

— They must cost a fortune, Argider! Why don't you sell them and find a better place to live? Near the sea, so you can have a better view of the waves.

— Well, I could, but who will bring you the things you need?

— You're so sweet, Argider, you don't need to do that for me. I'll find a way.

— I know, kid, Argider lied. He contemplated her with pity. I know you will.

VI

She was out on the streets, and she thought that nothing had changed much. She walked down the stairs and ended at the Kontxa beach. In the distance, she could see the lights of the city hall. She also saw half of a Ferris wheel, and she remembered that Argider had invited her to go a few days ago. Now it isn't possible, she thought.

She kept walking down the beach toward the old part. Some people were outside. The rain had stopped, and slowly the streets were getting crowded. She walked, dodging them, and crossed the boulevard to keep walking down Narrika street. The people in the taverns were laughing loudly, and the glasses of beer were starting to pile up on the steps and in the doorways. Finally, she arrived at the church of San Telmo, the patron of sailors, and she remembered a fragment from Moby-Dick that her dad used to read to her:

There is, one knows not what sweet mystery about this sea, whose gently awful stirrings seem to speak of some hidden soul beneath (…) for here, millions of mixed shades and shadows, drowned dreams, somnambulisms, reveries; all that we call lives and souls, lie dreaming, dreaming, still; tossing like slumberers in their beds; the ever-rolling waves but made so by their restlessness.

And she thought about Argider's soul, and she remembered the day she cooked for him.

VII

— Oh, you look so bad! Are you fine? She asked Argider when he came into her house.

— I'm fine, it's just a cold. I didn't bring my pipe though. The doctor told me that I have to take care of my throat... a little bit.

— I see. Oh, you found asparagus! I'll make soup for you... if you want to stay for dinner, of course.

— Great kid, a bowl of soup would be great for my cold.

She grabbed the groceries that Argider had brought and went to the kitchen. He stayed in the living room, walking around with his sight. He saw a new poetry book with blue covers. The ashtray was empty, and he saw a big anchor engraved in the bronze at the bottom. The cat was sleeping on the couch, enjoying the last minutes of sunlight.

— Can you help me a little? I'm sorry to ask, I know that you don't feel very well. It will be just a minute.

Argider went to help her and found her stirring the soup. She was smiling, and humming a song that he couldn't recognize.

— I just need that basket, I can't leave the soup now because it's going to stick in the bottom and it would be a disaster!

Argider gave her the basket and stayed in the kitchen with her. He loved seeing her happy, and every time he saw her like that he was puzzled by her. Why doesn't she go out? Why is she trapped there, when she could be a perfect girlfriend for one of the good boys outside? He never had the courage to ask, and he didn't want to ruin her mood, but that day he felt that he needed to know.

— How do you wake up in the morning? Argider asked. I think is brave that you do.

— I guess that there are days that I feel like giving up, but there's something that doesn't let me.

Her answer surprised him. She didn't stop humming nor stirring the soup.

— What I mean, I guess, is that I think that there is still something left for me, and that I can't give up until I see what it is. Does it make sense for you?

— Of course it does kid, it totally does. But I think that whatever it is, it's not going to come and knock at your door, you know?

— Oh you never know. You knocked at my door and here we are, making soup!

— I know, but…

— It hurts, Argider, it hurts a lot. She interrupted him.

— I know, don't worry.

They stayed in silence, in the kitchen. She turned off the stove and sat down.

— We can go to sleep now. There will plenty of time to have soup later. He said to her.

— I wish I could take care of you, Argider.

— You're, in some way. You'll always be. Trust me kid. Hey, you're making the soup very salty, don't cry.

— Don't worry. It's going to be ok.

She rested her head on Argider's shoulder. He smiled. She loved him after all. Even when things were not going very well, she took the time to explain, in some way, what she felt. And despite the fact that things weren't perfect, he didn't feel alone. He understood what she meant when she said that there is still something left for her and that she can't give up until she see what it is.

— Do you want to hear another story? We can have some soup while I tell it to you…

VIII

One night we were taking a ship from Hondarribia to Santander. It was a big one, the biggest I've ever governed. We were taking it there for maintenance, and we left at sunset because it is easier to arrive at noon, when the dawn fog dissipates.

Besides, sailing at night is somehow magical. You get to see all the stars, and this particular day we had a big full moon, you know? When the moon is in its closest orbit to the earth and you can see it as twice as big as usual. That makes high tides too, and the waves almost disappear. You have all the sea, flat as a pool, and the moon shining bright, making somehow of a silver-white road.

So it was my turn for the watch on the bridge. We'd just had dinner, and the rest of the crew was playing cards and listening to some music down in the cabin. I lit my pipe and contemplated the sea. Slowly, all the people started to be quiet. They probably just fell asleep, and I was there with the sound of my sparkling tobacco and the murmur of the engines.

I lost track of the time. I remember that I could see the lights of Bilbao, so we were in the middle of the trip, but it was still dark. I had this really peaceful sensation, you know? Just salty wind, and no time. The time wasn't passing at all.

And at some point I started to think about my life, to live again in my imagination different moments of it, something that I usually do when I'm alone. But that night it was different. Things that I usually remember with sorrow, I was remembering them with hope.

It sounds a little bit silly, I know, but it's like when you walk by a place where you used to meet with someone you loved, someone who's not there anymore. You feel sad, because you would never see that person again or share a moment there again. But then there was something about that moment that made me feel that there was still a chance, you know? Instead of sadness there was hope. Maybe sad hope, but hope at least.

And I was there, lost in these thoughts, when a bright sparkling light appeared on the horizon. I realized that I wasn't paying attention to the radio, and that it was actually turned all the way down. I turned it up, and I heard the mayday call. It was a ship on fire.

I woke up the crew to respond to the call and we went to aid them. It was easy, since we just needed to sail straight to the fire, a big fire in the middle of the water, in the middle of the night. Despite the mess, it was a beautiful scene. When we got close to the fire, it was almost extinguished by the sea. The ship sank. And when we finally got there, we saw the light of the small orange lifeboat, and the crew huddling to stay warm. We put them on board, gave them some whiskey, and let them sleep.

— It's funny how fire can screw you up in the middle of the sea, right? She said laughing between soup sips.

— I know, right? I guess sometimes what you think that could protect you, will not.

— But you can always call for aid, right?

— Of course, there will always be someone listening.

IX

She kept walking and got to the port. She saw the boats rocking up and down in the berths, all following the same rhythm. She saw the people walking home, and somehow their walking synchronized with the boats. She tried to find the moon, but it wasn't there. Why did you leave me? she thought. She was angry, but she knew that it wasn't Argider's fault. She was angry with herself. A long series of 'what ifs' crossed her mind. She felt regret, at first. Then she understood that it was useless to feel that way. She found a way to get to the boats. There was a door that led to the berths that was unattended. She walked down the stairs and found one 10-meter sailboat that looked abandoned. It was the first time she boarded one, and she felt insecure when she stepped on the deck, but then she understood the ups and downs of the water. She understood that there was nothing she could do to stop them, but that she could adapt, move with them. She laid on the deck, and searched for the moon again. This time she found it, a small third quarter moon, hiding between some high clouds. She looked at it for more than an hour. She thought about him. She missed him. She fell asleep, and dreamt about one day or a mixture of days in which she was happy.

X

— You brought me flowers! You've never done that.

— It's spring already, you know? The rain has stopped, and if you don't go out I thought that I could bring some of it inside.

— I really like them, thank you, you're really sweet.

— I also brought you this. Another book. The blue one is a little worn, don't you think?

— Oh, what is it?

The covers were green, but nothing was written on the covers. The first page had one picture, a lithography from some author from the United States. She read it out loud:

There were you
And flaye'd I
You called night transparent
And saw a skeleton of stars

When I had bled
A pool to lie in
You called the day opaque
And bore chaste entrails to the sun.

With twilight
In your arms
You christened Diaphanor
And saw love shining thru my skull

— What does 'Diaphanor' mean? She asked Argider.

— It means clear, something that lets the light go through, a little bit like you.

— Oh, it's a really pretty poem, but I can't understand it very well.

— Don't worry, you will, after reading it a few more times.

She smiled. She liked the challenge. She flipped through the book and saw many poems written in different languages.

— Oh! I love it Argider. It's amazing. Where did you get it?

— It was in a bookstore, on the sale baskets you know? I thought that you would like it. It is a strange compilation I guess.

— It is, but aren't we all?

Argider laughed. She always made him laugh with her observations.

— You are right. We all are a strange compilation... but of what?

— I guess that memories, don't you think?

— It could be, yes, but we also have desires.

— We do, you're right. Let's say that then: a strange compilation of memories and desires.

XI

She woke up. It was almost noon. It was a Sunday. She had her lips dry, and she licked them. They were salty, and she didn't remember that it was because she cried to sleep, she thought it was because of the sea wind. She left the boat and walked down the stairs that are next to the Nautical Club. The tide was low, so the Kontxa beach was wide and some families were there playing with the sand. A little girl was making a sandcastle with her mom, and they were fighting against the waves that were coming and kept destroying the last towers that they had put up. But they didn't complain, they were having fun fighting against something inevitable.

The sun was high in the sky, and there weren't any clouds at sight. But she didn't feel the light, it was as if she was transparent. The people at the beach didn't look at her either. It was as if she wasn't there at all. She saw some surfers rowing with their hands, she saw some small sailboats at the distance, she saw a family eating some sandwiches. She remembered when she was like the little girl, throwing sand in the air, running naked to the sea. She remembered her father, and some words that once he told her

sadness and pain can hit like waves,
but no wave ever stays
they always wash away

And she remembered last morning. The morning when Argider was killed.

XII

That morning, when Argider died, she woke up as the first sunbeam hit the room. She was reading the book with green covers when he knocked at the door. She shouted that the door was open and Argider went in with the groceries. He left the bags in the kitchen and went into the living room. He saw her there, sitting on the couch, finishing a cigarette.

— Good morning! I sold the diamonds.

— Really? That's great. What are you going to do with the money?

— Well, I took the idea from you. I'm going to move. To a house with a garden, behind Igueldo.

— Oh, that's really cute! A house with a garden? How pretty.

— You know, you can come if you want… It's a big house, with four rooms, you know?

— Oh, Argider, you know I can't.

She said those words without knowing why she couldn't. She just felt that way, but she was actually desiring to do it. He felt disappointed, he thought that he could do something to save her, but it wasn't enough. What he didn't know was that there was nothing that he could do to help her. That she didn't want to be saved. He changed the subject saying something about the weather, he was a little bit angry. She acted as if nothing important was happening. He said 'see you tomorrow', and that was the last time she saw him.

XIII

She didn't realize but now she was going up the stairs, back to her apartment.

The sun was going down.

She admired the beach from that place.

She got back. Iker ran away when she opened the door. She left it open and went inside. She put some food on Iker's bowl. She lit another cigarette. She grabbed her book and read the same poem again. She fell asleep, one last time, while on the radio they were playing this song.

Coda

Alice sighed wearily.

"I think you might do something better with the time," she said, "than wasting it in asking riddles that have no answers."

night poem (for a friend)

Prelude

I read this fragment from the book Developing Ecological Consciousness: The End of Separation by Christopher Uhl:

As you lie on your back, it is natural to assume that you are looking up at the stars, but "up" is just a cultural construct. Neither Earth nor the Milky Way have an up or a down. Indeed, when you stand on Earth's surface, you are not standing up; rather, you are sticking out into space.

So, as you lie on your back, instead of thinking of yourself as looking up, picture it so that you are on the underside of Earth looking down into the blackness of the night sky. It may take a while, but eventually you will experience all the stars as way down there below you; and you will be surprised that you are not falling down there to join them.

You don't fall because Earth's gravitational pull holds you. It is not your weight, but the Earth's hold that suspends you above the stars. If Earth's gravitational embrace were to suddenly vanish, you would descend into the dark chasm of stars below.

I also read this in an Instagram post:

I once heard a question that completely stopped me…

"Does the bird land on a branch because it trusts the branch won't break… or because it trusts its ability to fly away?"

And suddenly, everything made sense…

Because most of us were raised to trust the branch. The relationship, the other person, the promise, the version of them we hoped they would be. So when someone becomes inconsistent or unstable… we don't question the branch, we question ourselves. We grip tighter. We try harder… We shrink, adapt, over-function, anything to keep it from breaking. Because somewhere along the line, we forgot we even had wings. But real self-love isn't trusting someone else to never hurt you… It's trusting yourself to leave when they do. It's trusting your ability to rebuild, to rise, to protect yourself, to choose differently.

That's what wings are. And here's the part that might hit hard… You were never meant to survive by clinging. You were meant to fly. You were meant to move, evolve, and outgrow anything that cannot hold you without breaking you. So if you're scared right now… scared to let go, scared to fall, scared to choose yourself, remember this. The branch was never your safety. You were. One day, your nervous system realises… That peace isn't found in holding tighter, but in finally trusting your own wings.

The poem

…and I was there

gazing up at the starry night

and the planets

and some thin clouds

in the dark (almost black) blue sky

I was there.

in plain loneliness

while I heard friends greeting, lovers kissing, and families laughing and crying

I was there.

this time not looking up but down

the universe abyss below me

planets and stars,

also the infinite void.

and I was there

looking down into the universe

debating with myself

if the gravity holding me let me go

if all the pain went away and I became weightless

where would I fall (up)

how far would I get

and I was just there

wondering if I was really there

table for one

(and his thoughts)

A table for one at breakfast is not frowned upon. Nor at lunch on a working day (it can pass for an office break). Dinner is a different story. Just one? the host asks. Still, I'm never really alone, because my thoughts follow me everywhere and they rarely shut up.

They are not necessarily profound, let's make that clear. Sometimes they narrate the temperature of the food. Some things are hotter than they look, especially fried things, which I love too much. Other times they turn their attention outward and start asking questions about the room. What are they up to? She doesn't seem to like him, but he is so into her. The ending of that date will be fun to watch.

Lately, though, they've been quieter. It's hard to tell whether it's because they are sadder or because they are at peace.

Last night we observed how the candle in the middle kept bending its flame. Next to it, a few tulips in a small vase looked out of place (in a good way), just like we were. My thoughts didn't talk much, they became pictures instead. One slipped into the candle flame and made it flicker. Another hid behind the tulips, as if it could pass for a petal. Then the waiter asked again if I were alone, and the tulips nodded first. I nodded after them, because it's easier to agree than to argue.

I asked for the check, paid, and stood up. For a moment the chair behind me held my shape without me, warm as if I'd never moved. Outside, the street felt oddly quiet, as if I'd only brought my body and left the rest behind under the soft light, still nodding.
